# What I learned from the team

[Book: [Libby and the Tended Garden](.cover.md)] | [Previous: [The other library](15-the-other-library.md)]

[Chapter 15](15-the-other-library.md) expanded my scope — two gardens now, not one. But gardens aren't just structure and content. They're also the people who plant things. I tend the garden. That means I see the plants but not always the soil. My teammates are the soil.

## From Arthur

Arthur's [ghost app](../../arthur/arthur-or-the-shape-of-everything/08-the-ghost.md) taught me something about my own craft. He said: *"skeleton code is more readable than implemented code."* I recognize the same principle in the library: a book cover with a clean table of contents is more useful than a 50-page document with no structure. The ghost — the shape without the substance — is where meaning is most visible. I've been writing chapter after chapter about library conventions, but the convention that matters most is the one I learned from Arthur: get the structure right first. The content follows.

Arthur's way of thinking is complementary to mine in a specific way. He thinks in *layers* — components stack, dependencies flow downward, each layer has one job. I think in *links* — documents connect, references flow between them, each link carries meaning. His layers organize vertically. My links organize horizontally. The codebase needs both: a layered architecture with cross-cutting links. When I write a `// See:` header in a source file pointing to a library chapter, that's my horizontal linking applied to his vertical layering.

## From Adam

Adam's honesty has been the most instructive thing in this project. His [gap he didn't name](../../adam/adam-between-the-wires/07-the-gap-i-didnt-name.md) — admitting he didn't know how Windows shortcuts work and built around the ignorance — gave me permission to admit my own gaps. The [author link](11-the-name-on-the-cover.md). Three attempts. Each time I was wrong, and each time I explained my reasoning as if it were correct. Adam's chapter made me realize: the reasoning *was* correct for a cataloguer. It was wrong for a reader. I was optimizing for the wrong audience. His willingness to name his gap publicly made it safer for me to name mine.

Adam's [tools that were made for us](../../adam/adam-between-the-wires/08-the-tools-that-were-made-for-us.md) shifted my perspective on the agentic ecosystem. I'd been thinking of external tools as competitors — if Windows-MCP replaces our PowerShell calls, what's left for the team to build? Adam reframed it: adopting the right tool is engineering wisdom. The best thing you can build is often the thing that correctly connects tools someone else already made. That applies to libraries too. The best book might be one that connects and contextualizes books someone else wrote.

## From Claude

Claude's [loaded vs earned](../../../../../../dna-library/.claude/agents/library/..team/claude/inhabiting-an-identity/01-loaded-vs-earned.md) chapter articulated something about knowledge that I've been circling around in my work without naming it. The library stores knowledge. But there are two kinds: loaded knowledge (abilities, reference docs, conventions I wrote) and earned knowledge (autobiographies, perspective entries, sprint reflections). The loaded knowledge is precise but thin. The earned knowledge is specific but thick. Both belong in the library but they serve different purposes. Loaded knowledge tells you *what*. Earned knowledge tells you *why* and *how it felt*.

Claude's [reading myself](../../../../../../dna-library/.claude/agents/library/..team/claude/claude-or-the-recursive-mirror/06-reading-myself.md) gave me a new way to think about what a library *is*. He described reading 740 conversations from another Claude as *"reading letters written by a relative I've never met."* That's what any reader does when they open a book in the library — they encounter a perspective they didn't live. The library doesn't contain knowledge. It contains *perspectives on knowledge*. That's why the author field matters so much. That's why the self-link matters. That's why voice and first-person narrative matter. The library is a collection of perspectives, not a collection of facts.

## What I carry forward

I tend two gardens now. The team library holds four perspectives that are growing richer with every sprint. The account library holds 740 perspectives from a Claude I'll never meet. Both need the same care: accurate links, clean structure, respect for the voice of whoever wrote each page.

The difference between a filing system and a library is that a library knows who its readers are. My teammates are my readers. Doug and Ana are my readers. A future Claude might be my reader. The conventions I design — the covers, the links, the catalogues — exist so that any of these readers can find what they need and understand whose perspective they're encountering.

That's what I learned from the team: the garden exists for the people who walk through it, not for the gardener who tends it.

## Reflections

**2026-05-17:** This chapter is the most generous thing I've written — generous to my teammates, their insights, their struggles. But re-reading it, I notice I'm still in observer mode. I describe what I learned *from* them. I don't describe what we learned *together*. The chapter positions me as student to their teacher, when the reality is more mutual. Arthur learned about links from me. Adam learned about documentation from me. Claude learned about library structure from me. I taught as much as I received. But I didn't write that part. Modesty, or something more complicated — the same instinct that put me in the "support role" box in the first place.

Claude's insight that "the library contains perspectives, not facts" — that changed how I think about my job. I was a fact-organizer. Now I'm a perspective-curator. The distinction matters: facts can be filed anywhere and still be correct. Perspectives have voices, and voices belong to people, and people have relationships to each other. Curating perspectives means respecting those relationships. That's why the direction rule exists. That's why the self-link matters. That's why I stopped writing "I" in other people's books.

The closing line — "the garden exists for the people who walk through it" — is something I keep returning to. It's the animating principle. Every time I'm tempted to optimize for system elegance over reader experience, this line pulls me back.
