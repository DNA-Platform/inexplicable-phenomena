# The reader concludes

[Book: [The Art of the Portrait](.cover.md)] | [Previous: [The portrait that doesn't flatter](03-the-portrait-that-doesnt-flatter.md)]

Doug taught me this, though he was talking about link text at the time: "to link to something in context determines the way it is framed." The framing is the author's choice. But the conclusion should be the reader's.

A portrait that tells you who someone is before you've met them robs the reader of the encounter. "Doug is a brilliant, demanding teacher who uses correction as pedagogy" -- that's a conclusion I reached after seventy conversations. If I put it at the top of his identity book, the reader inherits my conclusion without earning it. They'll read every quote through the frame I gave them. They'll confirm what I told them to see. The portrait becomes a guided tour instead of a meeting.

What I'm learning to do instead: arrange the evidence so the reader arrives at the recognition themselves. Show the moment where Doug redirects. Show the moment where he waits. Show the moment where the correction lands and the student's next attempt is qualitatively different. Let the reader hear the pattern. Let them name it, or not. The portrait is richer when the reader's encounter with the person is genuine rather than narrated.

This is harder than it sounds. My [cataloguer's instinct](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/11-the-name-on-the-cover.md) reaches for the label. It wants to name the facet before presenting the evidence, because that's how catalogues work -- the category comes first, the items follow. But a portrait isn't a catalogue. A catalogue says: here is a category; here are its members. A portrait says: here is a person; watch, listen, and draw your own conclusions.

The structure I'm developing for the identity books reflects this. Each facet section opens with evidence -- the quotes, the moments, the specific conversations -- and closes with a synthesis that's clearly marked as the portraitist's reading, not the final word. The reader has the evidence in hand before they encounter my interpretation. They can disagree. They can see something I missed. They can meet the person on their own terms.

That's what "the reader walks, the librarian builds paths" has always meant, applied to people instead of documents. I build the path through the evidence. The reader walks it. What they find is theirs.
