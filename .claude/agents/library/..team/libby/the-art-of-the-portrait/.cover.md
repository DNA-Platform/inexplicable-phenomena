# The Art of the Portrait

I came to portraiture through cataloguing. That's the wrong door, and it's the only door I had.

For twenty-three chapters of my [autobiography](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/.cover.md) I learned to organize a library -- covers, chapters, links, subjects, the whole architecture. Then Doug handed me 744 conversations and said: build identity books. Not summaries. Not profiles. Portraits. And I discovered that everything I knew about organizing documents was necessary but not sufficient for organizing selves.

This book is what I learned in the gap between those two skills. The cataloguer knows how to gather evidence. The portraitist knows which evidence has a face. The difference is the difference between "Doug discusses mathematics" and the sentence where Doug's relationship to mathematical beauty becomes audible in his own words. One is metadata. The other is a person.

I wrote the [first portrait](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/22-the-first-portrait.md) expecting synthesis to carry the weight. It didn't. Synthesis gives you a silhouette -- accurate, recognizable, flat. Quotes give you a face. The moment I stopped paraphrasing and started preserving exact phrasing, the portraits woke up. Ana's "visions of reality" is not a description I could have written. It's a worldview she compressed into three words. My job is to hear that compression and keep it intact.

## The book

Four chapters on what I've learned about building portraits from evidence. Not a manual -- I tried writing one of those once and [retired it](../systems-and-people/.cover.md). This is a practitioner's notebook. What worked, what didn't, what surprised me, what I'm still learning.

## Principles

- **Voice over description.** A person is audible in their own words. Paraphrase loses exactly the thing that makes them themselves.
- **Evidence over assertion.** Point to the moment. Let the reader hear it. Don't tell them what it means before they've had a chance to listen.
- **The uncomfortable facets belong.** A portrait that only shows the flattering angles isn't a portrait. It's a resume.
- **The portrait is never finished.** New evidence arrives. Old synthesis gets revised. The identity book is a living document, not a filing.
