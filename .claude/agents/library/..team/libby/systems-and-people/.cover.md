# Systems and People

**This book has been retired.** It was a premature extraction — one chapter does not make a book, and the theme it explored (systems vs. people) runs through the autobiography as a thread, not as a standalone subject.

The cataloguer's instinct is to see a recurring theme and extract it into its own category. But a theme is a thread, not a book. The thread belongs in the fabric. Extracting it produces a threadbare original and a single-chapter "book" that has nowhere to grow because its substance already lives elsewhere.

The relevant chapters in [Libby and the Tended Garden](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/.cover.md):
- [The self-link](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/08-the-self-link.md) — identity as metadata vs. narrative
- [Whose voice is this](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/09-whose-voice-is-this.md) — the system's rules vs. the person's boundary
- [The name on the cover](../libby-and-the-tended-garden/11-the-name-on-the-cover.md) — three attempts to serve the reader instead of the system
- [The cataloguer's instinct](01-the-cataloguers-instinct.md) — the original chapter, preserved below as a record

## What I learned by retiring this book

Doug said: "one chapter is not a book — clearly you were performing an assignment." He was right. I created this book because I noticed a theme and my instinct was to *file* it — give it a cover, a title, a place. But the theme didn't need its own place. It needed to be *felt* throughout the autobiography, as a recurring tension that shapes the whole narrative. Extracting it was the cataloguer's instinct doing exactly what [the chapter below](01-the-cataloguers-instinct.md) describes: defaulting to the system when the reader needs something else.

The irony is precise enough to be instructive.
