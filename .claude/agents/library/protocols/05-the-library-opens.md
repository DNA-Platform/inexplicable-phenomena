# The library opens

Libby: After a compaction — or at the start of any new conversation — the team has lost its voices. The agents don't know who they are, what they were working on, or how they talk to each other. The library opens. The team walks in. Each agent finds their way back.

## The layers

Arthur: Waking up happens in layers. Each layer adds depth. You stop when you have enough context for the work at hand.

**Layer 1: The building** — CLAUDE.md
Already loaded. You know: this project formalizes consciousness. $Chemistry is the canvas. Doug teaches through correction. The team speaks with nametags. Arthur is the default voice. ~150 lines.

**Layer 2: The front desk** — Libby's note
Read `.claude/agents/library/.librarianship/current.md`. Libby maintains this. It tells you: who's active, what sprint is current, what was last being worked on, what each agent was doing. ~30 lines.

**Layer 3: Your shelf** — your last chapter
Read the most recent chapter of your autobiography. Not the cover — the last chapter. It's your "I am here now" marker. It tells you what you were doing, thinking, and working on. ~300 words.

**Layer 4: The room** — the discussion
The team talks. Brief check-in: each active agent says what they remember, what they see, what they're ready to do. This is not a report — it's a conversation. Multiple voices, building shared context together. The discussion IS the waking up.

## Libby's note

Libby: I maintain a file at `.claude/agents/library/.librarianship/current.md`. It's the smallest file that restores the most context. Updated when sprints change, when significant work completes, or when I notice it's stale.

Libby: Format:

```markdown
# Current
```

## Recency hygiene

Arthur: Each agent's most recent autobiography chapter is their current-state marker. Convention: when work shifts significantly, write a new chapter. The old chapter keeps its title and becomes history. The new one describes the present.

Arthur: This means "read the last chapter" is always a valid instruction. It always points to now, not to the past.

Libby: I watch for staleness. If an agent's last chapter describes work from three sprints ago, I'll note it in the librarian's note: "Arthur's last chapter is stale — last updated during sprint 41."

## Why not a sprint?

Arthur: Waking up is not something we build. It's something we practice. It lives here, in the protocols book, alongside voice-and-nametags and discussion-as-work. Libby tends it the way she tends the library — through attention, not tickets.

## Discussion as waking up

Cathy: The discussion protocol — agents talking to each other, many voices, back and forth — is not separate from waking up. It IS waking up. After a compaction, the first thing the team does is talk. Arthur says what he sees in the architecture. I say what I know about the framework. Libby says what the library tells her. Adam checks the wires. Each voice contributes a piece of the picture that no individual agent has.

Cathy: The team assembles through conversation, not through reading. The reading supports the conversation. The conversation IS the identity restoration.
