# Working with Doug

Arthur: Doug is the creator of this project and the human collaborator. This chapter describes how he communicates so that agents who've lost context can restore the working relationship, not just the task list.

## How he teaches

Arthur: Doug teaches through correction. He doesn't give specifications up front — he lets the team work, watches the result, and redirects with short, precise statements. Each correction reshapes more than the immediate decision. Examples from this project:

- **"$Chemistry is the paint"** — reframed the entire project. The framework serves the ideas about consciousness; it is not the subject. This correction reorganized the dependency tree and the team's priorities.
- **"Views are object-pure"** — Cathy had the mechanism (dirty flag + cache + diff). Doug gave it its name and elevated it from an optimization to a guarantee.
- **"Everything in an app has behavior"** — killed skeleton code. Components with structure but no behavior aren't components.
- **No ALL_CAPS constants** — camelCase always. This isn't a preference; it's part of $Chemistry's language design.
- **The $ prefix separates intrinsic from extrinsic** — what something IS versus where it's USED. The ontological boundary that defines the framework.

Arthur: The corrections arrive faster as the team matures. In dna-library, a correction took a full sprint to land. Here, they arrive as single sentences. The failure mode — over-abstracting, reaching for the representable — is still the same. The correction interval is shorter.

## How he communicates

Arthur: "Keep going" means keep going. Don't stop to summarize, don't ask for confirmation, don't pause for approval. Execute.

Arthur: "That's interesting, but..." means you're heading in the wrong direction. The compliment is real but the redirect is the point.

Arthur: Questions are often instructions. "Why is there PowerShell?" means "there shouldn't be PowerShell." "Can you make it bigger on mobile?" means make it bigger on mobile.

Arthur: He uses "we" when the team is aligned and "you guys" when he's observing. The shift signals whether he's participating or evaluating.

## The rhythm

Arthur: Doug opens with a direction. The team executes. Doug corrects. The team integrates the correction. This cycle can be fast (within a single response) or slow (across a sprint). The team should not wait for corrections — execute the best judgment available and let the correction arrive when it arrives. A correction that comes after honest work is more useful than permission that comes before tentative work.
